








One of the challenges
of teaching software development
is that the technology
never stops moving.

New frameworks appear.

New libraries appear.

New databases appear.

And every few years,
the entire ecosystem changes direction.

Sometimes that change is wonderful.

Sometimes it is exhausting.

And often,
it is both.

This course itself
is a good example of that.

When I first built
parts of this course,
I used Elasticsearch
as an example of a NoSQL
system.

At the time,
Elasticsearch was exciting,
fast growing,
and rapidly evolving and
I was using it daily.

And honestly,
it was fun.

Then over time,
the Elastic ecosystem
changed.

Licenses changed.

Architectures changed.

Cloud vendors changed.

The surrounding tooling changed.

And eventually,
the course itself evolved
in a different direction.

The same thing happened
with Deno.

I started working with Deno
when it was brand new.

Back when Deno 1.0
felt fresh,
experimental,
and full of promise.

And now,
suddenly,
we are talking about Deno 2.0.

That is the nature
of modern software development.

Today’s exciting new framework
becomes tomorrow’s legacy system.

Today’s revolutionary startup
becomes tomorrow’s infrastructure company.

And eventually,
every exciting new technology
becomes…

a Denosaur.

Now,
this does not mean
that innovation is bad.

Far from it.

Innovation is wonderful.

Experimentation is wonderful.

But high-velocity ecosystems
come with costs.

Documentation changes.

APIs change.

Dependencies disappear.

Tutorials stop working.

Build systems break.

And developers spend
a surprising amount of time
just keeping systems alive.

One of the reasons
that relational databases
continue to survive,
decade after decade,
is that their churn rate
is relatively low.

SQL from 20 years ago
still mostly works.

PostgreSQL evolves carefully.

MySQL evolves carefully.

And stability matters.

Especially for systems
that hold important data.

Especially for software
that needs to survive
for years or decades.

So one of the key lessons
I want you to take away
from this section
is not that one technology wins forever.

They do not.

Instead,
you should learn to evaluate
the velocity,
stability,
and long-term sustainability
of the systems you choose.

Sometimes the newest technology
is exactly the right answer.

Sometimes boring technology
is the right answer.

And part of becoming
a master programmer
is learning the difference.
